# Sprint Goal Writing

## What a Sprint Goal Is

A sprint goal is a single, concise statement of what the team intends to achieve by the end of the sprint. It is outcome-oriented, not task-oriented. A well-written sprint goal answers the question: "Why is the team doing this sprint's work?" — not "What tasks will the team complete?"

The sprint goal is the one commitment the team makes during sprint planning. Individual stories may be adjusted during the sprint; the goal should not be. If a goal becomes unachievable mid-sprint, that is a signal worth a mid-sprint conversation with stakeholders.

## Outcome vs Task Goals

**Task-oriented goal (bad):** "Complete the login page, the password reset flow, and the email verification story."
This is a task list, not a goal. It provides no cohesion, no business context, and no basis for trade-off decisions during the sprint.

**Outcome-oriented goal (good):** "Enable new users to register and log in for the first time."
This is cohesive, business-relevant, and guides decisions. If the email verification story turns out to be blocked, the team can ask: "Can we still achieve the goal without it?" Often, the answer is yes — and that is exactly the kind of conversation a good goal enables.

## The Sprint Goal Formula

A reliable structure for sprint goals: **[Action verb] + [who benefits] + [so that / enabling outcome]**

Examples:
- "Give prospective customers a way to self-serve a demo so that sales can qualify leads without a call."
- "Enable project managers to export reports so that they can share status with executives."
- "Harden the checkout flow against payment failures so that we reduce cart abandonment by half."

The "so that" clause connects the sprint to business value and makes the goal defensible in stakeholder conversations.

## Connecting Goals to Outcomes

Sprint goals should trace upward to product outcomes and product outcomes should trace to company objectives (OKRs or equivalents). Before finalizing a sprint goal, confirm:

1. Which product outcome does this sprint advance?
2. How will we know the sprint succeeded? (Can the goal be verified objectively?)
3. Is this the most impactful use of the team's capacity this sprint?

A sprint goal that doesn't connect to any stated product outcome is a signal that the team is either building the wrong thing or that the product roadmap is not connected to business objectives.

## What Makes a Bad Sprint Goal

**Too vague:** "Improve performance." Performance of what? By how much? For whom?

**Too broad:** "Build the payments module." This is likely three or four sprints of work compressed into one goal — it will fail or mislead.

**Implementation-focused:** "Integrate Stripe and refactor the cart service." This describes technical tasks, not outcomes. Reframe: "Enable users to complete a purchase using a saved payment method."

**Multiple unrelated goals:** If a sprint goal has the word "and" in it, it probably contains two goals. Multiple goals fragment the team's focus and make it impossible to declare the sprint succeeded or failed.

## Sprint Goals and Sprint Reviews

The sprint goal is the lens through which the sprint review is conducted. At the review, the first question should be: "Did we achieve our sprint goal?" — not "Did we complete all the stories?"

A sprint where 80% of stories are done but the goal is achieved is a success. A sprint where 100% of stories are done but the goal was changed mid-sprint is a failure worth investigating.

## Involving the Team

Sprint goals written by the product owner alone tend to be more task-oriented and less owned by the team. Sprint goals co-created during sprint planning — even if the product owner comes with a draft — generate more team commitment and better trade-off decisions during the sprint.

Invite the team to challenge the goal draft: "What would make this goal unachievable?" "Is this the right goal given what we learned last sprint?" These questions surface hidden dependencies and sharpen the goal before work begins.

## Goal Tracking During the Sprint

Post the sprint goal visibly in the team's sprint board or digital workspace. During daily standup, briefly reference it: "Are we on track to achieve the goal?" This keeps the goal alive rather than letting it become forgotten context. A team that forgets its sprint goal by day three did not have a meaningful goal.
